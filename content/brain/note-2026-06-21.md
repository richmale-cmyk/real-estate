I've signed contracts that looked like victories and turned into operational nightmares. I've also approved agreements where finance questioned my sanity, only to watch them deliver outsized value for years.

The procurement paradox: optimising for unit cost often maximises total cost.

Ground handling is where I learned this lesson repeatedly. A handler bidding 15% below market isn't hungrier or more efficient — they're either desperate or lying about what they'll actually deliver. Six months in, you're firefighting daily service failures, burning management hours, damaging your on-time performance, and eventually paying for the rebid anyway.

What I look for now:

**Operational fluency over polished proposals.** Can their station manager articulate exactly how they'll handle an A321 turnaround when two flights land within fifteen minutes? Generic answers reveal generic thinking.

**Pain tolerance indicators.** How do they respond when I introduce irregular operations scenarios? The ones who get specific about recovery protocols have lived through chaos. The ones who promise "full support" haven't.

**Reference asymmetry.** Skip the references they provide. Find airlines who left them. Those conversations tell you everything.

**Escalation architecture.** Who answers the phone at 3am when a flight is delayed and crew are timing out? "Our operations centre" is not an answer. I want a name and a direct number.

The real cost of a bad contract isn't the money — it's the attention debt. Every hour I spend managing a failing supplier is an hour I'm not spending on strategic problems. Multiply that across 83 stations and you understand why I'd rather pay more for reliability.

Procurement isn't purchasing. It's selecting who you're going to be in a relationship with for the next three to five years. Treat it accordingly.
