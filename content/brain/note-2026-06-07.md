I've signed hundreds of ground handling agreements across four continents. The lesson that took longest to learn: procurement isn't about getting the lowest price. It's about buying the right risk profile.

Early in my career, I celebrated when we squeezed a handler down 15% below their opening offer. Six months later, we were dealing with chronic understaffing, missed turnarounds, and a relationship so adversarial that every service failure became a contract dispute instead of a problem to solve together.

The paradox is this: aggressive negotiation often destroys the operational margin your partner needs to actually deliver. You win the spreadsheet battle and lose the tarmac war.

Now I negotiate differently. I spend more time understanding their cost structure than arguing about price. What's their staff turnover? What's their equipment replacement cycle? If the numbers don't work for them, they won't work for me — I'll just discover it later, at 2am, when a flight is delayed and phones aren't being answered.

The three questions I ask before any procurement decision:

1. **What happens when something goes wrong?** Not if — when. Will this partner absorb the shock or amplify it?
2. **What am I actually buying?** A service, yes. But also a relationship, a communication style, a problem-solving culture.
3. **Where's the hidden subsidy?** If a price looks too good, someone is paying for it. Usually through quality. Sometimes through their staff. Eventually through my operation.

The vendors I've worked with longest aren't the cheapest. They're the ones who told me no when I asked for something unrealistic. That honesty is worth more than any discount.

Procurement is just decision-making with a signature line. And like all decisions, the quality shows up downstream.
