There's a counterintuitive truth I've learned running procurement across 83+ stations: the hardest negotiators rarely get the best outcomes.

I used to think procurement was about leverage. Squeeze the vendor. Multiple bidders. Aggressive timelines. And yes, competition matters. But the deals that actually perform — the ones where service levels hold, where the relationship survives disruption, where you're not renegotiating every six months — those come from a different place.

The paradox is this: making yourself easier to buy from often gets you better terms than making yourself harder to sell to.

What does that mean practically?

It means clear specs that don't change mid-tender. It means payment terms you actually honour. It means telling a ground handler exactly what "performance" means before they sign, not after they fail. It means being the customer that vendors want to keep, not just the one they're stuck with.

I've watched competitors beat suppliers into submission on price, then wonder why equipment mysteriously becomes "unavailable" during peak season. Meanwhile, we're paying 3% more but getting priority allocation when it matters.

The real cost of procurement isn't on the purchase order. It's in the exceptions, the escalations, the operational friction when a partner decides you're not worth the extra effort.

This doesn't mean being soft. It means being precise about what you need, fair about what you'll pay, and ruthless about enforcing what was agreed. Vendors respect clarity more than aggression.

The goal isn't to win the negotiation. It's to build a supply chain that doesn't need constant renegotiation.

Every hour I spend fighting with a vendor is an hour I'm not spending on something that actually moves the operation forward.
