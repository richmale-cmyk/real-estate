Any operation looks good in steady state.

The real test is what happens when things go wrong — at 3am, with a full aircraft, a skeleton crew, and a handler who's already had two difficult turnarounds that shift.

I've seen stations that run perfectly during audits and fall apart the moment an observer leaves. The standard wasn't embedded — it was performed.

The difference between a station that holds and one that doesn't isn't resources or headcount. It's whether the supervisor on the floor has genuinely internalised *why* the standard exists, not just what it is.

You can write a checklist for everything. You cannot write a checklist for judgment.

The work is building people who make good decisions under pressure, not people who follow procedures when it's easy.
