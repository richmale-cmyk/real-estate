I've sat through hundreds of procurement cycles. Ground handling contracts, equipment tenders, service agreements across dozens of countries. And here's what I've learned: the process often exists to protect the decision-maker, not to produce the best decision.

We build elaborate scoring matrices. We weight criteria to three decimal places. We create evaluation committees so no single person bears responsibility. Then we pick the vendor we were probably going to pick anyway, but now we have a paper trail that proves we were "objective."

This is procurement theatre.

The uncomfortable truth is that good procurement requires judgement — the kind that can't be fully documented in a spreadsheet. You're evaluating whether a supplier will actually show up at 3am when things go wrong. Whether their regional manager has the authority to solve problems or just escalate them. Whether their pricing is sustainable or a loss-leader they'll claw back through change orders.

None of this fits neatly into weighted criteria.

What I've started doing differently: I still run the formal process (you have to), but I also run a parallel track. I call references the supplier didn't provide. I visit their other operations unannounced. I ask their frontline staff how decisions get made internally. I watch how they treat the people serving us coffee in meetings.

The formal process tells you what they want you to see. The informal process tells you who they actually are.

The best procurement decisions I've made came from trusting pattern recognition built over years, then using the formal process to validate and document — not the other way around. The worst came from hiding behind process to avoid making a call.

Rigour is not the same as rigidity.
