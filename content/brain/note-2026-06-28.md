I've watched procurement teams celebrate "savings" that cost the operation millions downstream. Ground handling contracts are the perfect example. You squeeze a handler down to razor margins, then act surprised when they can't retain staff, when turnaround times slip, when your on-time performance craters.

The math looks simple on a spreadsheet. It never is on the ramp.

What I've learned managing 83+ stations: the real cost of a vendor relationship lives in three places the RFP process ignores.

**Recovery capacity.** When things go wrong — and they will — does this partner have the depth to surge? The handler who bid lowest often did so by running lean. Lean breaks under pressure.

**Hidden coordination tax.** A cheaper vendor with worse systems means your team spends more time managing them. That's invisible cost. I've seen station managers burning hours on calls that should have been handled by competent partners.

**Switching friction.** The true cost of a bad vendor includes the eventual cost of replacing them. Transitions in ground ops are brutal. New handlers take months to reach baseline performance. Passengers feel it. Crews feel it.

My procurement framework now weights operational resilience at 40% of the decision. Not because I don't care about cost — I run a budget like everyone else — but because I've learned that reliability has compounding returns.

The vendors worth keeping aren't the cheapest. They're the ones who call you before you call them. Who have a plan when weather hits. Who invest in their people because they're not operating at the edge of viability.

Price is what you pay. Performance is what you get. The gap between them is where operations lives or dies.
